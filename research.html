Graduate research

Laser cooling

Principal investigator: Professor Lan Cheng


Physicists can use lasers to cool down atoms and molecules to near absolute zero, which makes them easier to study. Compared to atoms and diatomic molecules, polyatomic molecules are much more difficult to cool with lasers because of their additional vibrational degrees of freedom. But polyatomic molecules are a promising option for precision measurement, and it has been proposed that they could play a vital role in the search for physics beyond the Standard Model. I am constructing a potential energy surface fit for one of these polyatomic molecules, which will then be used to figure out how the laser cooling can be performed.


Light-induced conical intersections

Principal investigator: Professor David R. Yarkony (deceased)


In 1995, it was said that "Ever since the development of the laser, the quest to use light to control the future of matter has been one of the Holy Grails of chemistry". Light–matter interactions can give rise to light-induced conical intersections (LICIs), as the dipole–field interaction can significantly change the topography of the coupled potential energy surfaces. A conical intersection is a geometric orientation where two electronic states of a molecule have the same energy. They are important in photochemistry because they facilitate ultrafast radiationless decay, which occurs in human vision, DNA photoprotection, and photosynthesis. This work explored how a LICI affects the the product distribution of ammonia photodissociation. A Floquet Hamiltonian was used to implement the light interaction, and quantum chemical data was provided by diabatic potential energy, nonadiabatic coupling, dipole, and spin–orbit coupling matrix surfaces fitted using neural networks.

Photodetachment spectroscopy simulation

Principal investigator: Professor David R. Yarkony (deceased)


Anion ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy is a helpful tool that can provide a great deal of insight into the vibrational behavior of molecules. Usually, it is relatively easy to simulate a molecule's spectrum: just calculate the Franck–Condon overlaps. But this all falls apart for cyclopentadienyl because its lowest electronic states cross (a conical intersection). Since the states are strongly coupled, a more advanced technique is required to perform the simulation. This is known as the time-independent approach to the multimode vibronic coupling approximation. I used this approach to study tetrazolyl, which like other azolyls, is closely related to cyclopentadienyl. Tetrazolyl's complex and difficult-to-calculate electronic structure necessitated the use of a model with fourth-order terms, even though other azolyls could be studied using only second-order parameters.

Published in The Journal of Chemical Physics
Presented at the 28th Dynamics of Molecular Collisions Conference


Undergraduate research

Microscopic Characterization and Elemental Mapping of Catalytic Aerogels

Advisor: Professor Mary K. Carroll (2024 ACS president)


Automotive catalytic converters contain platinum-group elements, the mining of which is environmentally destructive. Metal-containing aerogels are a promising alternative thanks to the unique properties of aerogels including their high specific surface area and heat resistance. I synthesized metal-infused aerogels using a hydraulic hot press with a patented rapid supercritical extraction method. I characterized nanostructure by operating scanning electron microscopy (SEM), energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS), powder X-ray diffraction (XRD), atomic force microscopy (AFM), and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC), all of which I operated myself. With conventional protocols, SEM is nearly impossible on aerogels because they are so insulating, and AFM is difficult because aerogels are so soft. I developed and implemented novel sample preparation techniques that enabled nanometer-resolution microscopy of these tricky materials.

Liquid-Liquid Interfacial Assembly of Peptoid Sheets

Advisor: Professor Ellen Robertson


Gold nanoparticles exhibit localized surface plasmon resonance, which changes their color depending on their environment and how close they are to each other. This is promising for sensing applications, including the detection of environmental toxins. I synthesized peptoid monolayer sheets containing gold nanoparticles and used UV–Vis spectroscopy to assess localized surface plasmon resonance activity.

Published in Chemical Communications
Presented at the 30th Steinmetz Symposium
Presented at the 259th ACS National Meeting


Calculating Surface Tension with Capillary Waves

Advisor: Professor Jef Wagner


I constructed an experimental apparatus to measure the surface tension coefficient of liquids. A wave-generating motor produced capillary waves on the surface of the sample liquid. To make the measurement, image tracking software was used to examine the dispersion relationship of these waves.

Presented at the 29th Steinmetz Symposium
